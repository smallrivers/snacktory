Mindfulness tips to skip workplace stress this silly season

  • Share
  • Share via Email
  • Share on Google Plus
  • Post on facebook wall
  • Share on twitter
  • Post to Linkedin
  • Share on Reddit
by Tom Loncar

December is again upon us, a time when many workplaces engage in a frenetic range of "winding down", end of year reporting and Christmas activities.

Auto-piloting (or auto-limping) is standard practice for many, but a more mindful approach can help you meet the challenges more fully, authentically and enjoyably.

What is mindfulness… and why now?

Jon Kabat-Zinn, founder of the Mindfulness Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) program at the University of Massachusetts Medical Center says:

"Mindfulness means paying attention in a particular way, on purpose, in the present moment and non-judgmentally."

Rather than passive neutrality, it is purposeful attention and our reactions to this attention that are the keys to realising any benefits. It diverts us from instinctive "autopilot" tendencies that emerge in response to a stress trigger, whether it be a direct report's sullenness or a lack of acknowledgement from a colleague.

Kate Mathers, principal mindfulness trainer at the Institute of Executive Coaching and Leadership, where she delivers the eight week MBSR program, says that mindfulness is particularly relevant in most corporate settings in December. "With mindfulness training we get much more skilled at noticing our own reactivity – and December's often frenetic, relentless pace gives us additional opportunity to explore that pause, that one second gap that paying close attention to our own reactivity affords us, enabling us to move towards a considered, thoughtful response instead."

While the roots of mindfulness programs are based on meditation practice, programs such as MBSR are secular and practical in nature, and supported by a rapidly growing scientific evidence base illustrating a diverse range of benefits, including increased focus, less emotional reactivity and higher cognitive flexibility.

While there has recently been a backlash to an emerging "McMindfulness Movement", this has not prevented leading corporates such as Ford, Google, Target and Adobe recognising the impact that mindful employees can have to their bottom line, and implementing their own programs.

So, at a personal level, what is there to be gleaned from mindfulness this festive season? Here are four ideas worth considering.

Fresh eyes

"The real voyage of discovery consists not in seeking new landscapes but in having new eyes," is a quote attributed to French novelist Marcel Proust. Taking a fresh look at things can take you in surprisingly pleasant directions.

It is not easy though. Unfortunately, our capacity to evaluate even the mundane events that come our way as "not being ideal" can divert us from encountering the moment as it fully is.

So what might happen if you divert from autopilot and be more mindful? The office Christmas party may reveal a surprising connection with a person who you may otherwise dodge or bypass after superficial pleasantries. A Christmas card signing may emerge as an opportunity to say something real to a client or supplier who deserves your gratitude.

Reducing judgement, appraisal and comparison, and instead presenting a more open mind may reveal many unanticipated surprises. Your new and open eyes will help you be a better "noticer" – a skill with both personal and professional relevance. As leadership guru Warren Bennis said, all of the best leaders are also "first-class noticers".

Not drowning, surfing

Opening up also extends to the more routine but stressful compliance-based December demands that emerge. The last few weeks of the year may throw up wave upon wave of reporting obligations. Avoidance or grinding acceptance can be the norm, but as Jon Kabat-Zinn said "you can't stop the waves, but you can learn to surf".

Forget having an idealised working environment; the waves are there. Your more open outlook may reveal some diminutive but potentially precious nuances within.

Savour, don't shovel

Christmas offers ample opportunities to indulge. Big time. Auto-piloting food and alcohol intake is an often present and tempting slippery slope at Christmas time. There is, however, a mindful and more enjoyable alternative. Indeed, eating and drinking are prime targets for mindfulness practice.

The early stages of Kabat-Zinn's MBSR training introduce the mindful wonders associated with the humble raisin. Mathers says "the raisin meditation is the first example of the central tenet of the MBSR mindfulness program: relearning how to bring awareness to everyday activities so that we can see life as it actually is, unfolding moment by moment. And once you see the difference that paying full attention makes to the small things in life, you start to get an idea of the cost of inattention – all the pleasures of seeing, hearing, tasting, smelling and touching all passing by us unnoticed."

The culinary delights associated with Christmas offer a range of opportunities for mindful imbibing. Smell and seek out the floral notes that are purportedly embedded in your Sav Blanc. Find and relish the toffee apple and creamy malt flavours that the makers of Fat Yak beer believe are there. Latch on to your foodie friend for the Christmas buffet and delight in your combined discoveries. From curt shovelling to connoisseurial savouring, a side serving of mindfulness can help.

and if all else fails…

As clichéd as it may sound, breathe. Breath-attention is a key part of mindfulness practice. Attention to it can delay and indeed sidetrack any looming autopilot response. A technique proposed by many mindfulness practitioners is the short STOP technique. Here are the steps:

  • Stop: In response to the experience that presents, deliberately pause and check on your thoughts, feelings and sensations, and register them, including the uncomfortable.
  • Take a breath: After stopping, direct all your attention to your breath, following each in-breath and out-breath as they cycle. This action can ground you back to the present.
  • Observe: From the inwardly oriented breath, awareness is expanded outwardly to your body and what is happening around you. Observe with curiosity.
  • Proceed: Following your successful interruption of autopilot, and the space you have engaged, you can now proceed and reconnect with your activity. And potentially opening to new possibilities.

December presents an ideal time for engaging with whatever emerges with the best you have to offer. 'Tis the season to be jolly. And mindful.

Tom Loncar is an executive, leadership and career coach who helps his clients develop strategies for thriving in complex environments.

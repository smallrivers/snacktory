Virgin Galactic co-pilot Michael Alsbury from Scotts Valley

By Samantha Clark, Santa Cruz Sentinel

SANTA CRUZ -- Test pilot Michael Alsbury who died Friday in the crash of the Virgin Galactic space plane grew up in Scotts Valley.

The SpaceShipTwo's re-entry system deployed early before crashing Friday, according to investigators.

Christopher Hart, the acting chairman of the National Transportation Safety Board, said the rotating tail was unlocked by Alsbury, but moved, too early for the plane's speed, into the feathering position "without being commanded."

Hart said in a press conference Sunday that the early deployment of the feathering system, which rotates the tails to create drag and ease re-entry into the atmosphere, is a "statement of fact and not a statement of cause."

The investigation is ongoing.

Michael Alsbury was killed while co-piloting the test flight of Virgin Galactic SpaceShipTwo on Friday. (AP Photo/Scaled Composites)

Pilot Peter Siebold, 43, was injured in the crash and managed to survive by parachuting to the ground.

Co-pilot Alsbury, 39, died. The Scotts Valley native lived in Tehachapi with his wife, Michelle Saling, and two children ages 10 and 7.

Alsbury worked for Scaled Composites, a firm working with Virgin Galactic to develop private spacecraft, in Mojave for 15 years as a project engineer and test pilot.

Alsbury received a bachelor of science in aeronautical engineering from Cal Poly San Luis Obispo. He and his twin sister, Nikki Alsbury Levy, graduated in 1993 as the valedictorians of Soquel High School. He also attended Scotts Valley Middle School and Vine Hill Elementary School and was an Eagle Scout and played soccer.

He is the son of Richard and Linda Alsbury of Scotts Valley.

Childhood friend Darren Knipfer of San Jose remembers Alsbury's room being filled with model airplanes.

"He stuck to his path," Knipfer said. "Everyone has their dreams as a kid. How many people grow up to be what they wanted?"

Alsbury's middle school math teacher David Huemoeller of Ben Lomond said he was a nice young man and a good student.

"He was always interested," he said. "You'd love to have a class full of kids just like him."

Charlene McKowen of Santa Cruz was a favorite teacher who taught him Spanish at Soquel High School. She remembers his work ethic, intelligence and earnestness.

"Everything about him, anyone would be proud to have in a son," she said. "He was kind beyond words."

A memorial fund in his name raised more than $55,000 in one day. For more information, visit http://www.gofundme.com/MikeAlsbury. ------ (c)2014 the Santa Cruz Sentinel (Scotts Valley, Calif.)

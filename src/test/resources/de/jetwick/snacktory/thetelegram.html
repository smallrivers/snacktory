Fundraiser response overwhelms family

Glen Whiffen
Published on November 24, 2014
  • Share 0
  • 0 Comment

Arrangements being made to bring crtically ill man home by air ambulance

Only days after fundraising officially began to raise $40,000 to transport Paul Byrne home to Newfoundland from Ontario by air ambulance, the goal was surpassed.

And Byrne’s wife, Carolyn Ford, is overwhelmed by the generosity.

“Our friends and family in Newfoundland, throughout Canada, Asia and the U.S. have demonstrated such a powerful force of kindness and generosity that we are forever in their debt,” she said in a statement.

“The simple word ‘gratitude’ just doesn’t capture what I am experiencing. What I know for sure is that the people of Newfoundland are a clan that backs you up — no matter who you are or where you are. Paul is a proud Newfoundlander and it is our privilege to call Newfoundland our home.”

Paul Byrne, 42, a former assistant coach with Memorial University’s men’s and women’s varsity basketball teams, was diagnosed with Stage 4 Prostate Cancer in 2013 while living in Brantford, Ont. Through his battle with cancer since, Byrne has continued coaching basketball and continued to be an inspiration to those around him.

In September, just three days after returning to his home in Brantford following surgery, he developed a blood clot in his lung. He was transported to St. Joseph’s Hospital in Hamilton by ambulance and two days later, while still in hospital, went into cardiac arrest due to another blood clot.

Since then he has been in what is termed as a “persistent vegetative state” — a condition that has more lower-brain function such as breathing, heart-rate regulation and sleep, and a bit more upper brain-stem function like eye opening or making sounds, than a person in a deep coma. The person can be released to the family for home care.

After only being in Brantford a relatively short time, it was felt best for Byrne to be transported to Newfoundland to be close to a wide circle of family and friends where there would be much more support for Ford and their daughter Rebecca, 9.

Ford noted that when Byrne’s sister, Jennifer, suggested fundraising they had no idea what the following days would bring.

Melissa Batchilder, a friend of the family, said when it became clear that Byrne would need long-term care, it was obvious the best place would be in Newfoundland.

“What began as a Facebook group to raise awareness and solicit positive messages to read to Paul became a fundraising force when it was determined he should be moved home,” Batchilder said. “It took on a life of its own this weekend when $40,000 was raised in less than three days to ensure Paul Byrne makes it home by Christmas.

“Through a social media campaign, private donations and the Sea-Hawks, the funds were raised at an unpredictable speed. Funds were donated at all levels from east coast hamlets to cities. Mary Browns and the Williams Family Foundation both stepped up with contributions, and the Sea-Hawks also donated proceeds from its 50-50 draw at Saturday’s game, which also saw the winner donate his cut.”

Batchilder said, in the days ahead, final preparations will be made to airlift Byrne home to an appropriate health-care facility in St. John's.

Paul Byrne is originally from Gander. His parents, J.J. Byrne and the late Clara Byrne, were both teachers. Paul was a popular assistant coach for the MUN Sea-Hawks basketball teams in the 1990s and early 2000s before moving to Asia. The family returned to Canada settling in Brantford in 2011. His father, many relatives and friends live in the St. John’s area.

See website at: http://www.gofundme.com/BringPaulByrneHome.

Organizations: Stage 4 Prostate Cancer, Hospital in Hamilton, Sea-Hawks Williams Family Foundation

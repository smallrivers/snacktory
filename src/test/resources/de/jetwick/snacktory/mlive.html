      'Christmas Wishes Tour' party bus brightens the holiday season for Essexville family

      Print
      Sam Easter | seaster@mlive.com By Sam Easter | seaster@mlive.com The Bay City Times
      Email the author
      on December 22, 2014 at 3:54 PM, updated December 22, 2014 at 5:54 PM
      View/Post Comments

       

      Christmas Wishes Tour members sing carols Members of the 10th annual Christmas Wishes Tour sing carols at Cartwright Hospice in Saginaw Township and the Seidenstucker residence in Essexville Sunday, Dec. 21.
      ESSEXVILLE, MI — More than two dozen carolers shuffled into Kari Seidenstucker's Essexville home on Sunday, bearing smiles, gifts and the promise of a merry Christmas.

      On its final stop of a "Christmas Wishes Tour," the crowd was fresh off a long, black party bus that had already made calls throughout the Great Lakes Bay Region, including stops at Covenant HealthCare and Hoornstra Adult Foster Care Home in Saginaw.

      The trip didn't diminish their enthusiasm, though, and after 15 minutes of carols and a few tearful hugs, Seidenstucker was left next to a mound of presents and food in the small apartment she shares with her two daughters.

      "It was wonderful and overwhelming at the same time," Seidenstucker said, sitting on the edge of her bed as her 2-year-old attempted to assemble a new dollhouse. "It just amazes me that help out there is real."

      The carolers, presents and party bus were all the work of a group of friends led by Bay City resident Lauretta Kloha, who organizes the annual tour, now in its 10th year. Kloha and her gang of do-gooders make visits in the Bay City and Saginaw areas — to families, nursing homes and hospitals — all in the name of giving presents and spreading good cheer.

      "My dad was always really generous with Christmas gifts, and I asked him (in 2005) for a limo bus — all I wanted to do was give back," Kloha said. "I felt the need, I felt as though I was being directed to help others or those in need. I thought, 'How great would it be to go sing Christmas carols to random strangers and put faith back in humanity again?'"

      For Seidenstucker, the gifts couldn't have come at a better time. She was diagnosed with Hodgkin's lymphoma in early 2013, a development that ended her jobs as a waitress and bartender as she began a six-month battery of treatments, a gap she has since filled with odd jobs ranging from babysitting to house-painting. Though she's now cancer-free, she was diagnosed in late 2013 with trigeminal neuralgia, a nerve condition she said leaves her with difficult facial pain.

      Other troubles have also added up recently. Her car's rear window shattered while she was using her defroster, and the replacement bill has put her behind on rent. Doctors found masses in her abdomen less than a week ago, though Seidenstucker said she doesn't yet know if they're cancerous.

      "This woman, when I asked her for her wishes… it was just things that have to do with the quality of life with her two daughters," Kloha said of setting up her group's visit. "Like a dining room table, because she misses having dinner with them. In the 10 years I've been doing this, I've never had anyone so beautifully express what they want."

      Kloha added that her group finds families in need in a variety of ways — sometimes from groups like the United Way or Make A Wish, other times through personal recommendations. Seidenstucker was referred to the group by a friend who noticed a GoFundMe account set up by Kari at gofundme.com/hmc518.

      As for presents, Kloha said that everyone on the bus helps pitch in. 

      In the past decade, her group has impacted a slew of families.

      She recalls helping the family of a solider who was recently slain in Afghanistan. Another time, the group showed up at a household, but no one was home. The group managed to open the garage door and leave piles of presents inside.

      "Can you imagine their faces?" said Shelley Vincent, a close friend of Kloha and co-organizer of the tour.

      Kloha and her group impact more than families. This year, the bus made a stop at Cartwright Residence, a hospice in Saginaw Township, where the group sang carols and visited with residents, entering one room to hold the hand of a woman living at the facility.

      "Even though she can't verbalize it, you could tell by the smile on her face that she enjoys it," Diane Glasgow said. "I think it means a lot."

      Over the past decade, the Christmas Wishes Tour has grown. Saginaw area-based Mobile Boogie Party Bus now provides free transportation for the event. Kloha plans for about 30 people to participate, nearly double the inaugural tour's attendance. After the first year, her friends started pitching in to help plan the event, which she said includes a good amount of coordination.

      Kloha said most of the participants are her family and friends, but some new faces have joined along the way. If anyone would like to join the event next year, Kloha said they're welcome to contact her at 989-714-6612.

      Emalee Seidenstucker, 5, opens a gift after members of the Christmas Wishes Tour left Sunday, Dec. 21 in Essexville. More than two dozen people filled the apartment to drop off gifts for the family. (Yfat Yossifor | The Bay City Times)Yfat Yossifor 

      One of the evening's most notable attendees was Ambrosia Macdonald, a 24-year-old Carrolton Township resident whose home was visited by the tour when she was 17 years old. She discovered the group by chance, finding a photo of herself and her siblings while sifting through Facebook photos of a friend who was involved with the group.

      "It's different," she said of reversing roles. "To be able to give back, it's definitely uplifting."

      At the end of the night, 5-year-old Emalee Seidenstucker sat with pieces of a new doll house at her feet, and a pile of presents nearby. Shyly, she mentioned how many presents there suddenly were, and how it made her feel:

      "Happy."

      — Sam Easter is a general assignment reporter for The Bay City Times. He can be reached at seaster@mlive.com.

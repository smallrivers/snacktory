What Happens to Stocks During Government Shutdowns?

Answered by
By
Answered by
Randy Frederick
Managing Director of Trading and Derivatives, Schwab Center for Financial Research
- September 28, 2015

Key Points

  • Government shutdowns can create anxiety, but historically little else.
  • Any volatility spikes are likely to be relatively short-lived.
  • There are strategies to mitigate near-term market volatility, but a simpler approach may be to ignore the noise and focus on your long-term goals.

Every year, prior to October 1, Congress must approve a budget for the next fiscal year—or at least some sort of stopgap funding bill or continuing resolution to keep the government operating. Failure to do so can result in a government shutdown, with a direct impact on federal employees and federal contractors and ripple effects on a variety of companies that do business with the government.

Unfortunately, because the two major political parties often have very different views on where and how government funds should be allocated, the budget frequently becomes a hostage in the political process. Points of contention change every year, but in 2015 some of the hot topics included:

  • Funding of Planned Parenthood
  • Military spending increases
  • Domestic spending increases
  • Recent congressional approval of the nuclear agreement with Iran
  • Raising or suspending the debt ceiling again
  • Replenishment of the Highway Trust Fund
  • Tax provision extensions
  • Proposal to repeal the carried-interest deduction for hedge funds

Government shutdowns can cause tremendous market anxiety. However, they have not typically had a strong negative impact on stock market performance. Let’s take a look at some historical data on this topic.

Below is a table showing all 17 government shutdowns that have occurred since 1976. As you can see, the impact was negative for the stock market only about half the time, and the decline never exceeded 5%. While past performance is never a guarantee of future results, there is simply no historical precedent for a government shutdown causing major calamity in the equity markets.

Note: Equity performance is represented by the S&P 500 Index (SPX). Source: Schwab Center for Financial Research.

Past performance is no guarantee of future results.

Data as of 09/25/2015.

While a government shutdown is likely to raise the anxiety level of an already volatile market, history tells us that it is unlikely (though not impossible) for it to result in a significant market decline.

You may notice in the table above that the 2013 shutdown resulted in the largest gain (+2.4%) among the 17 shutdowns. However, it didn’t start out that way. Six business days after the shutdown began, the S&P 500 Index was down by 1.6%. But then rumors began to leak out that a deal was being struck in Washington, leading to the biggest single-day rise (+36.16 points) since the first day of 2013. That rally was followed by additional gains in three of the next four sessions, for a total of +39.97 points (+2.4%) overall since the shutdown began.

Reviewing the broader economic picture from previous shutdowns also generally revealed no negative impact. For example, nonfarm payrolls grew by 200,000 in October 2013, up from 175,000 in the previous month, surpassing expectations for growth of 120,000. In November 1995, they increased by 148,000, followed by an increase of 132,000 in December 1995. Other economic data released around the time of the 2013 shutdown was mostly either neutral or positive, too.

Because a government shutdown could result in government employees or contractors losing their jobs, you might wonder if it would spill over into weekly initial unemployment claims. Looking at the trend in initial claims following the eight shutdowns that exceeded a week (the minimum time required before you can file a claim), I found that in most years, there did appear to be a small spike a week after each shutdown. However, the spike was no larger than those seen at other times, and it usually reversed within a few weeks after the shutdown ended. In other words, it is impossible to know if any of the spikes were related to the shutdown or if they were just statistical noise.

Government shutdowns do cause uncertainty, and one way to measure uncertainty is the Chicago Board Options Exchange’s CBOE Volatility Index® (VIX® Index). As the VIX was created in 1993, it has existed through only the last two government shutdowns. While there was no noticeable uptick during the 1995 shutdown, as you can see in the VIX chart below, there was a sizable uptick during the 2013 shutdown. But even then, it was no greater than the mid-year uptick caused by comments from then-Federal Reserve Chairmen Ben Bernanke regarding when the Fed might start tapering purchases in its bond-buying program (an event often referred to as the “taper tantrum”).

Source: StreetSmart Edge®. Data as of 9/25/2015.

If the prospect of uncertainty, and possibly higher volatility, induced by a government shutdown worries you, there are a number of protective strategies that can be used to hedge this type of event.

However, a simpler approach may be to just ignore the near-term noise and focus on your long-term goals. History shows that no government shutdown during the past 40 years has lasted longer than three weeks, and that any long-term effects have been largely insignificant.
